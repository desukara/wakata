Tokyo Stories

I figured I understood Tokyo.
Tokyo had other ideas.

Little mistakes. Quiet corrections. The kind nobody bothers to spell out for you.

Start with the mistakes Read the rules afterward

The start

I thought I knew the basics.

Be polite. Be quiet. Don’t act strange.

Simple.

I made it maybe 48 hours.

After that, it got very obvious: I was not adjusting.

I was taking part incorrectly.

The stories

Nothing dramatic happened. That was the issue.

The Train

First real test: the train.

Instant silence.

Not regular silence. More like: did I miss some announcement?

So I adjust. Lower my voice. Keep it respectful.

I’m thinking—yeah, I understand this.

I do not understand this.

Because in Tokyo, if you’re adjusting your volume, you’re already off.

The move is not speak more quietly.

The move is don’t speak.

At all.

Which would’ve been nice information maybe ten seconds earlier.

I say one sentence.

A normal sentence. Nothing outrageous.

Somehow, I’m the loudest thing happening on the train.

Nobody looks. Nobody responds.

Which is worse.

Because now I’m the only person there who has no idea what’s going on.

Fully grown. Talking. On a Tokyo train.

Amazing.

The Escalator

Next lesson: escalators.

Easy. Stand. Ride. Mind your own business.

I can do that.

I step on. Stand on the left.

Feels correct. Comfortable. Confident.

This is me now. Tokyo guy.

And then nothing happens.

That’s the issue.

Because behind me, there’s a line.

Not moving.

Not complaining.

They just accept that I have decided to stop progress.

And I don’t notice.

I’m up there relaxed, enjoying the ride.

Meanwhile I have created a quiet traffic jam with my entire body.

Nobody says anything.

Of course they don’t.

They let you discover your mistake like an adult.

Slowly. Internally. Completely against your will.

The Convenience Store

Convenience store.

Easy.

I’ve done this before.

Grab. Pay. Leave.

I get to the counter.

The cashier is moving fast. Clean. No wasted motion.

I respect that.

So I jump in—thank you.

Early.

Too early.

Now I’m ahead of the moment.

Which is not where you want to be.

I say it again.

Trying to recover.

That makes it worse.

Now I’m doing too much.

Meanwhile the cashier hasn’t changed at all.

Still smooth. Still precise.

Like I’m not even required.

Which... may actually be right.

The Sidewalk

Walking in Tokyo looks normal.

People moving. I can do that.

I slow down for one second to check my phone.

That’s it. One second.

And suddenly—I’m in the way.

Completely.

Like I placed myself there on purpose.

People adjust.

Not dramatically. Just enough.

Which tells you everything.

Because now it’s like—oh.

This isn’t random.

There’s a rhythm.

And I stepped out of it.

For one second.

That’s all it takes.

The Restaurant

Restaurant.

Sit down. Menu. Easy.

I’m ready this time.

Watching. Waiting. Learning.

The server comes over.

I order clean. Confident.

This is better. I can feel it.

Small pause.

Something’s off.

Not big. Just slightly wrong.

And that’s worse.

Because now I don’t know what it is.

Too early? Too direct? Not enough? Too much?

Everything felt right.

Which is usually the problem.

The food comes. Everything is smooth.

And I’m sitting there like—yeah... I’m still missing something.

The adjustment

It’s not one mistake.

It’s every one of them.

Small things. Quiet things. Things nobody explains.

You just feel it when you get them wrong.

Usually a few seconds after the fact.

What happens next

There is a system here.

You’re already inside it.

You just can’t see it yet.

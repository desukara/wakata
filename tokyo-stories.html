The Telepathy Train

Tokyo Story · 5 min read

Nothing in life can really prepare you for the total silence in Japan. I’m talking about the ultimate quiet game. Not library tone or even that “hey, can you lower your voice please?” quiet. Not even close. This is organized silence on a national level. Japanese train rides make you feel like you walked into a board meeting you were never invited to, and everybody had already agreed, “We’re simply not talking at all today.” And I’m standing there wondering… when was the group chat? Why didn’t I get the message? When did Tokyo decide we’re going full pin drop?

I step on the train and the doors close, very quietly. Right away it’s extremely obvious… nobody’s talking. No coughing or little throat clearing noises… not even a silent fart. I’m looking around sure that there must be a valid reason for this, maybe some sort of sign, something in English… did I miss a clue? You know they say a lot over those announcement speakers in Japanese… so I’m wondering, did that translate to “please remain silent until further notice”?

But what really throws me off is how comfortable everybody seems to be. Nobody seems tense or worried, and no one is signaling that anything is wrong. Nothing even happened to justify this level of silence. And nobody thinks it’s strange at all. This is just… normal. Silence is apparently part of the transportation system that comes with the ticket.

So I adjust as I think to myself, no problem, I can respect this. Cultural awareness and samurai level discipline. I’ll just adapt to the environment. So I’m looking out the window, watching Tokyo glide by, modern skyscrapers and neon signs, everything moving so silently it feels like a Charlie Chaplin movie without subtitles. And I lean over a little, very quietly, whispering to my wife, right in her ear… “yeah… we should probably get Burger King later… maybe McDonald’s…” I even feel good about being so culturally aware. I’m being respectful and appreciating another way of life.

But I didn’t understand the point of this quiet game. I really didn’t. So I whisper again, I laugh… and then I get that strange feeling. You know that feeling you get when you realize… somebody is watching you?

I look up, and there’s this old man just staring right at me. Not a casual glance either. I’m talking about the death stare. Ice cold and not shy about it. I deliberately broke a social rule nobody needed to explain because everybody already knows it. And now I’m thinking what’s the big deal, I’m whispering, what’s this guy’s problem? But here’s what I didn’t know… if you’re whispering, you’re already too loud. The rule isn’t “try to keep it down.” The rule is… “don’t make any sound at all. Silence is golden and that’s the standard.”

So at this point, I’m feeling like… alright Tokyo. We’re done adapting.

I make eye contact with the old man across the train… and I say one sentence.

“Hey, we’ll switch to telepathy.”

Just one sentence of completely normal communication. Maybe a little more enthusiastically than I had intended.

And somehow… I become the problem.

Not a small one either. The whole train reacts to this. People look up from their phones like, “what was that?” Somebody else starts talking… in Japanese. Then another person comes back to life. Then a couple more. Still quiet, but now there’s sound and life. Progress in it’s early stages. I triggered something and it turned into a domino effect. Suddenly speech was spreading out all over the train.

And the strangest part? Nobody actually looks at me during any of this. Except the old man. He stays mad the entire ride and keeps glancing at me in disapproval. When we reached his station he gets gets off the train, shaking his head. Then he stops and turns back, looks at me through the window, and gives me one last look of shame.

…I wave goodbye to him.

That’s how you know you’re in a different world.

Because where I’m from, somebody would have at least laughed or, at the worst, said something sarcastic. There would be some kind of verbal response. Here? Nothing but silence and quiet judgment. And that’s worse. Because now I’m standing there with my wife, clearly not blending in, talking… with absolutely no feedback from anyone.

And now she’s getting the look because she’s supposed to know better. I can get away with it, as an outsider. But her? No. She’s got no excuse. Now she’s looking at me with scorn in her eyes.

But then something interesting happens on the train. People start talking more. At first it’s just the kids, then even the adults start chatting with each other. Because once the old man got off the train, the pressure left with him and everyone relaxed a little. Conversations start popping up, and eventually nobody’s holding back.

So I’m standing there pondering… did I just override the system?

I believe I unlocked speech on a muted train.

Now I’m really wondering… what else is possible? Do I stop here? Do I eat rice with a fork or walk inside with my shoes on? No… that feels like way too much, even for me.

But it really makes you think.

I guess I never realized silence could be so loud.

Because on a Tokyo train… one quiet sentence… suddenly feels like surround sound.

The Cat Crosswalk

Tokyo Story · 5 min read

Walking to 7-11 in Tokyo feels like stepping into a perfectly organized system where everyone already understands the rules… except you. People move in step, everything’s coordinated, and it all looks completely rehearsed. I can walk too. I’ve been doing that my whole life. This should not be that complicated… or so I thought. I stop for one second to check Google Maps, just one small second, and wham…suddenly I’m in the way. No one bumps into me or complains. They simply adjust to the situation. And that’s what makes it uncomfortable, because it becomes crystal clear this isn’t random. There’s a steady rhythm here, and I’m not part of it. Tokyo is marching to a drum beat that I can’t hear, and I stepped out of it for a second. That’s all it took. One second, and I went from being a person walking to being a big obstacle that needs to be avoided. Nobody says anything at all, but you can see the flow moving around you in a single file line, and at the same time you start to admire how precise it all is. Then you step aside and wait for the chance to jump back in.

Now I am paying careful attention. Everyone is on the left side, moving in a geometrically perfect line, not drifting or sloppy, and without hesitation. It’s not fast but it’s very exact. So I try to match the pace. Stay on the left side, keep a steady pace, leave some space between you and the person in front of you, and don’t get in the way. Finally, I get in where I fit in, and it actually starts to feel kind of manageable. Then an orange and white cat comes out of a side street and walks right in front of me. There wasn’t any hesitation or awareness of anything around it, it just merged into the line. And I think, well, now everything is going to get crazy. It’s a wild cat walking around a society obsessed with social discipline.

But nothing disorderly happens. The cat joins the line of pedestrians and I’m the only one who was surprised by any of this. It stays on the left side, moving carefully in the same direction, keeps the same pace, the same social distance. So, I find myself walking behind a cat as if this is a completely normal part of the system. And it is!

At that point, I’m trying to understand what I am seeing, because this is not how cats should behave. They don’t follow human social structures. Cats don’t acknowledge rules. This cat looks like it has somewhere to be. It has purpose. So I watch it closer, because I already know no one back home would ever believe this unless I make a video and post it on FB. We arrive at a crosswalk. It’s early in the morning, around six o’clock, middle of the week. There are no cars at all, so the street is completely empty.

Yet everyone stops.

The cat stops too!

And this is where it becomes difficult to process. The cat is standing almost at the line, leaving proper space, waiting with everyone else. As if that wasn’t enough… no one reacts to this, not even slightly. No one wants to bother the cat or insult it’s dignity by singling it out. It’s treated as entirely normal, as though this cat is just another participant in the Japanese system. And I completely understand people following rules, that makes sense. But a cat following rules raises some serious questions.

Because there are no cars… at all.

Where I’m from, that situation has a much different outcome. In America, a crosswalk is more of a guideline than a requirement. If there are no cars, you just cross the street. If a police officer happens to notice, you might acknowledge them, and that’s usually more than enough. The expectation is really simple. You check both directions and then you go. There is no reason to wait if there is no danger. Especially if there are no cars on the road at all.

But here in Japan, no one moves without permission.

Not one single person… or cat.

Everyone stands there ever-so calmly, as if time itself is paused until the signal changes from red to green. The rule was settled and Tokyo is ruled by the rules. And so, I’m looking at this tiger-striped cat, trying to understand why it’s participating in something it doesn’t even need to follow. It has no legal obligations or consequences to face. It’s entirely free to leave at any moment and walk or climb anywhere.

And yet, it waits at the crosswalk until the light says “go ahead.”

Eventually, the light does turn green. Everyone starts walking again, and the cat walks right along with them, keeping it’s position. I’m still behind it the cat, following like an undercover cop, walking through the crosswalk in the same rule-following manner.

And at that point, I’m no longer thinking about the Tokyo. It’s the comparison that stays on my mind. Japan is so very different.

I stopped for one second and disrupted everything. This law abiding cat entered the system, followed every rule, and made no mistakes at all.

I’m just trying to understand how things work.

But even the cat is better at this than me.

Hot Sauce Apologies: They Put Sorry on Everything

Tokyo Story · 6 min read

Listen… have you ever gone somewhere and realized… people are apologizing profusely… I mean, everywhere and all the time? Because this one caught me completely off guard. I’m just a regular American man, okay? I moved to Tokyo to be with my wonderful Japanese wife, thinking I understood how this world works. You see, in my hometown, apologies are not handed out lightly. You don’t just throw “I’m sorry” around like free samples at Costco. Not at all. An apology back in America is a serious milestone. Something had to go wrong before you get to that point. There’s a whole story, maybe eye-witnesses are involved. Because somebody did something blame-worthy, then felt something, and then stepped forward to take accountability for their actions. That’s what “I’m sorry” means to Americans. It carries a lot of weight. It’s not a causal “excuse me.” It’s a lot closer to repentance than politeness. So imagine my confusion when I get to Tokyo and people are apologizing… constantly. Not because something even happened or because somebody messed up. Just… off gp. General principle. Sorry in Tokyo is built into conversation like punctuation marks.

I’m walking down the street, minding my own business, and somebody passes me with more than enough room to move around me comfortably in the same hardware store aisle, “sumimasen.” That means sorry. And I’m thinking… sorry for what? You didn’t push me or even disturb my personal space. And then it keeps casually happening more often. At train stations, walking on sidewalks, in grocery stores. I go into an Ito Yokado, reach for some rice, and I try it myself, “sumimasen.” And it so feels… nice and smooth. As if I just tapped into some social operating system I didn’t know was there all along. Then somebody walks past me, “sumimasen.” I say it back, “sumimasen.” Now we’re both apologizing for being there in the same square footage. And nobody did anything wrong at all. At that point, I’m just realizing, hold on… what are we even apologizing for anymore? We’re just in it now because this is an apology loop.

See, back home, if you apologize too much, people start getting suspicious real quick. You say “sorry” three times in a row, somebody’s going to stop you and ask, “Hey… what did you do?” Now you’re under investigation, and you didn’t even commit a crime. And in the USA, there’s this unspoken rule… if you apologize too much, you look soft. Nobody wants to be known as a people-pleaser because they fold under pressure. So we don’t use “I’m sorry” lightly. We might say “my bad,” sure, that’s used casually, but even that doesn’t mean repentance. That’s just a quick acknowledgment, and it’s the closest thing to the vague Japanese apology we’re referring to here. But “I’m sorry”? That’s a lot heavier and closer to “I repent” than “excuse me.” But in Japan, they use apologies like hot sauce and put it on everything.

And the crazy part about it is, nobody looks or sounds guilty. There’s not any tension in it because it’s smooth, almost like breathing. And that’s when it finally clicked, and I understood this isn’t about guilt. It’s about saying, “I see you, recognize that I exist in your space, and I don’t want to disturb your peace.” Think of it as preventive maintenance or emotional insurance. Outside Japan, we wait until something goes wrong and then deal with it before it gets out of hand, sometimes not politely. But here in Tokyo, they handle problems before they become situations. They even smooth the moment before it has a chance to resemble a situation, wrapped up with deep bows and polite tones.

Then I find out for myself… this isn’t just one apology level. There's a whole lot more to this Japanese apology system. I didn’t know that at first. I thought sorry was just sorry, point blank. If you’re thinking, “Oh, sumimasen is just how Japanese people say excuse me”… you’re not exactly wrong, but it’s much deeper than that…because sumisamen is more like the pre-apology. Fasten your seatbelt because we’re picking up the pace now. They have different levels of apologies with their own procedural mini systems attached. You’ve got the light one, “sumimasen,” that’s the pre-apology, and it’s more like excuse me, sliding past somebody lingo. Then you’ve got “gomen nasai,” okay, now you actually did something that needs to be addressed before a situation developes. You’re late for an appointment, you forgot it was your turn to bring coffee, you inconvenienced somebody at the mall. Then it escalates, “moushiwake arimasen.” Now you’re in professional territory of apologies. That’s workplace accountability, and we need to talk about this one before HR gets involved. It's like… customer service voice activated. Then you go a little bit deeper, “taihen moushiwake gozaimasen.” This time, you’re saying something profound and you need to take it seriously. You’re bowing with sincere intention of humbling yourself in front of the person you did dirty. And now, ladies and gentlemen, comes the final level, known as the public apology. This one is the long bow, the repeated statements of ultimate regret, when the whole company’s standing there looking at you like, “We have reflected on this and have learned a valuable lesson, we will do much better moving forward,” or someone’s getting fired. And I’m really amazed by all this since… y’all got apology choreography, Tokyo! This was all carefully structured by somebody with a lot of forethought and psychology skills.

But here’s what really made me slap myself, it works every time. There’s no steady buildup of tension or lingering resentment over the years. No small thing turning into a big thing and everybody wondering who pushed the red button. Everything just flows because everybody has already accounted for the possibility of inconvenience. In the good old US of A, somebody steps on your new shoes, it’s a moment. You’ve got choices, America. You can choose to just ignore it, react on the spot, escalate without thinking, or learn from the Japanese. Out here in Japan? Those special moments dissolve instantly like Tang. The little problem never becomes a situation in the first place. And I’m living with the realization now, if everybody’s apologizing and nobody’s doing anything wrong… nothing even gets to the problem level, and it definitely never hits the situation level. There’s checks and balances all along the way. In Tokyo, everything feels smoother, and nobody’s offended. But even if they are offended, they at least have procedures set in place to prevent escalations. You can be mad if you want, but so and so said the right apology and bowed at the right angle. Life goes on.

And I had to re inventory my own apology process, because I came from a place where apologies felt like being soft. It meant giving something up you had to earn, which some refer to as street credit. But in Samurai Land, it’s not surrender at all. The apologies are another part of how this society stays in harmony. It’s the same harmony as standing quietly on the train, walking on the correct side, waiting at the crosswalk even when no cars are coming. This whole Japanese social ecosystem is all inter-connected with rules from the ancient ninja days. And somewhere along the path in my adventures through Tokyo, without even realizing it, I stopped refusing to apologize and fighting everything and started apologizing to stay synchronized. And once I understood apologies on a higher level, there was nothing left to fight about.
